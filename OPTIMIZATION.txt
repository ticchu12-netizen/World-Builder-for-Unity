# Making It Run Fast

The tool can place thousands of objects. That looks great, but without a few settings,
all those objects can slow your game down. The good news is that most of the speed
comes from one or two simple toggles. This guide goes from "do this first" to
"advanced, only if you need it."

You do not have to do all of this. Start at the top. Most projects only need the first
two things.

---

## 1. Turn on GPU Instancing (do this first, biggest win)

This is the single most important setting. It lets the graphics card draw hundreds of
copies of the same object in one go, instead of one at a time.

How to turn it on:

1. Find the materials your models use.
2. Click a material.
3. In the Inspector, tick the **Enable GPU Instancing** checkbox.

Do this for every material your scattered objects use. If you used the asset prompt and
shared one material across your models, you only have to do this once. This is why the
prompt asks for a shared material: it makes instancing easy and your scene fast.

If your scene is slow and you only do one thing from this guide, do this.

---

## 2. Mark scenery as Static (second biggest win)

Objects that never move can be "batched" together by Unity, which means fewer draw
calls and better speed.

After you generate, the tool puts everything under a "WORLD_GENERATED" object in your
scene. To mark it all static:

1. Click WORLD_GENERATED in the Hierarchy.
2. In the Inspector, top right, tick the **Static** checkbox.
3. When it asks, choose **Yes, change children** so everything inside gets marked too.

Now Unity can batch your scenery. Note: only do this for things that do not move. Your
player and enemies should NOT be static.

---

## 3. Use the GPU Resident Drawer (Unity 6, great for big worlds)

If you are on Unity 6 with URP, there is a modern feature that draws huge numbers of
objects very efficiently. It is built for exactly this kind of scene.

To turn it on:

1. Find your URP settings. Edit > Project Settings > Graphics, then open your
   Render Pipeline Asset, or look under Quality settings for the URP asset.
2. Look for **GPU Resident Drawer** and set it to **Instanced Drawing**.
3. There is usually a related setting to enable it on your renderer too. Turn that on.

This works best together with GPU Instancing from step 1. On a big generated world, this
can be a large speed boost. If you are on an older Unity version and do not see this
option, skip it. Steps 1 and 2 still carry you a long way.

---

## 4. Keep object counts sensible

The simplest speed lever is just placing fewer things. You rarely need a tree every few
feet for a scene to look full.

In the tool:

- **Density**: lower it. A lot of forests look great at 0.3 to 0.6.
- **Scatter Budget**: this caps the total scattered objects. Keep it at a number your
  machine handles. If you hit the cap, the Console tells you.
- **Path Tile Spacing**: bigger spacing means fewer path tiles. On large maps this
  matters a lot.

Generate, look, and only turn density up if it still looks sparse and your frame rate
is fine.

---

## 5. Add LODs for distant objects (advanced)

LOD means "Level of Detail." It swaps a model for a simpler, cheaper version when it is
far from the camera, and hides it entirely when it is very far. For a big open world
this is a real win, but it takes setup and you need simpler versions of your models.

This is optional and more advanced. If your world runs fine after steps 1 to 4, you do
not need it. If you have a very large, very dense world and still need more speed, look
up "Unity LOD Group" and add LOD Groups to your heaviest models (usually the trees).

---

## 6. Turn colliders off where you do not need them

Colliders cost performance, both to create and to run. The tool lets you turn them off.

- While building and previewing, keep **Add Colliders** off. You do not need collision
  to see if the layout looks good.
- When you do want collision, you often only need it on the big things: trees, walls,
  rocks you bump into. You do not need a collider on every blade of grass or flower.

---

## A simple plan for most people

1. Turn on GPU Instancing on your materials. (Step 1.)
2. Generate your world, then mark WORLD_GENERATED as Static. (Step 2.)
3. If you are on Unity 6, turn on the GPU Resident Drawer. (Step 3.)
4. Keep density reasonable. (Step 4.)

That covers the vast majority of cases. Steps 5 and 6 are there for when you are pushing
a really big world and need every last bit of speed.

---

## How to tell if it worked

Open the Game view and turn on **Stats** (the button in the top right of the Game view).
Watch the **Batches** number. Lower is better. After turning on GPU Instancing and Static
batching, you should see that number drop a lot compared to before. If it is still very
high, double-check that your materials actually have instancing ticked and that the
scenery is marked static.
